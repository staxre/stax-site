Whether you're acquiring your first net lease investment or selling a gas station you've operated for decades, understanding the transaction process is essential. Here's a step-by-step walkthrough.

## 1. Define Your Goals

Before entering a commercial real estate transaction, establish clear objectives. Sellers might seek to unlock capital through a sale-leaseback, optimize their portfolio, or retire from operations. Buyers typically pursue income-generating assets, portfolio diversification, or 1031 exchange replacement properties.

## 2. Choose the Right Partner

In the intricate world of commercial property transactions, having the right partner is essential. Look for a brokerage with deep specialization in your asset class. For gas stations and NNN properties, that means working with someone who understands fuel volumes, environmental due diligence, and lease structures.

## 3. Market Research and Property Selection

Buyers should analyze location fundamentals, traffic counts, fuel brand strength, and lease terms aligned with their investment criteria. Sellers benefit from understanding current market conditions, comparable sales, and optimal timing.

## 4. Due Diligence

This is where deals are made or broken. Comprehensive property assessment includes financial history review, environmental site assessments (Phase I and Phase II), lease analysis, and risk evaluation. For gas stations, underground storage tank compliance and fuel supply agreements are critical.

## 5. Negotiation

Securing favorable deal terms requires expertise and market knowledge. From purchase price to closing timelines, inspection contingencies to tenant estoppels — every term matters.

## 6. Financing and Documentation

Buyers obtain financing through lender relationships while sellers prepare sale-leaseback agreements, title work, and closing documentation. Having experienced counsel and a knowledgeable broker streamlines this phase significantly.

## 7. Closing the Deal

The final stage involves fund transfers, ownership transition, and document processing. A well-organized closing ensures no last-minute surprises and a smooth handoff.
